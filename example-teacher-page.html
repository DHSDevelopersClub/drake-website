AP English Language and Composition: Spring 2017

Teacher: Greg Doherty: gdoherty@tamdistrict.org

Onward we go in this college-level class. It is still divided into three major skill sets:

Rhetorical Analysis: the ability to describe writers’ strategies and devices, with evidence.

Argument: the ability to employ these strategies and devices to support your opinion on something.

Synthesis: the ability to read (and evaluate) a bunch of sources on a complex subject and come to a compromise or unified position. We'll usually combine this with the argument skill set: supporting your argument with good sources. 

We practice rhetorical analysis primarily through essays and multiple-choice tests. The proficiency scale is here.
We practice argument primarily through frequent essays. The proficiency scale is here.
We practice synthesis through unit essays and practice exams. The proficiency scale is here.

We continue to cycle through all of these in several units over the course of the semester.

These can be translated into percentage scores through normal math (3/4=75%). However, these percentages, as a semester average, translate into letter grades as follows:

Rules on Late Work, Revisions and Extra Credit

You may turn in missing assignments up to a month late for reduced credit. Eligible late work is accepted until the end of your final exam. It will not be accepted after your class’ final or during vacation or the next semester.

Within a month after getting it back, you may revise work that scored 3 or below for a higher score. Revisions will not erase late penalties. Work revised within 2 weeks of being returned to you can earn a maximum of a 3.5; work revised within a month of being returned can earn a maximum of a 3 (so you are rewarded for doing it right the first time or at least revising it promptly). Revised work and extra credit are not accepted during the last two weeks of the semester (Dead Week and Finals Week). See me for extra credit opportunities.
